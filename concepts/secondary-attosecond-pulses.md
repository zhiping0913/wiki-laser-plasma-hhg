# Secondary Attosecond Pulses

## Overview

Under certain conditions, the primary attosecond pulse generated by relativistic HHG is followed by a **weaker secondary pulse** after a delay of approximately one plasma period (~100-500 as). This has important implications for:
- Isolated attosecond pulse generation (secondary pulses contaminate isolation)
- Spectral interpretation (modulations in the spectrum encode pulse separations)
- Understanding of plasma dynamics after the primary emission

## Observations from PIC Simulations

### Spectral Modulation ↔ Multiple Pulles

Two adjacent finite pulses produce a decaying spectrum modated at intervals related to their separation. Edwards demonstrates that **strong spectral modulations correspond to secondary attosecond pulses**:

- Modulation frequency ω_m corresponds to pulse separation 2π/ω_m
- At S = 6.4, the delay between primary and secondary pulses depends strongly on a₀ and N
- For large N: modulation period increases, secondary pulse becomes relatively smaller

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.1 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 1953]
  "strong modulations in the reflected spectrum of a single-cycle incident pulse correspond to the generation of multiple attosecond pulses per cycle"

### Delay: ~One Plasma Period

The delay between primary and secondary pulses is approximately the inverse plasma frequency:

**Δt ≈ 2π/ω_p** where ω_p = √(N) · ω_L (in boosted frame: √(N/cos³θ_L))

The spectral modulation maxima occur at:

**ω_m/ω_L = √(N/cos³θ_L)** for n = 1, 2, 3, ...

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.1 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 1970]
  "ω_m/ω_L = √(N/cos³θ_L)"

### Secondary Pulse Intensity

The ratio I_{a,2}/I_{a,1} can be as high as **0.7** — the secondary pulse may not be negligible!

At a₀/N ≈ 0.12, the two pulses actually **switch**: below this value, the N-driven secondary pulse is the stronger one. At exactly a₀/N = 0.12, two pulses of equal magnitude are produced.

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.1 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 1980]
  "Near a₀/N = 0.12, the two pulses actually switch: below this value, the stronger pulse arrives after the weaker pulse"

## Physical Mechanism: Electrostatic Plasma Response

### Primary Bunch Formation
During the primary attosecond pulse emission:
1. Laser field drives electrons into a dense nanobunch
2. Bunch emits synchrotron-like radiation (primary pulse)
3. Fast electrons penetrate into the plasma body

### Charge Imbalance → Secondary Bunch
After primary emission:
1. Fast electrons create charge imbalance behind the bunch
2. Electrostatic plasma field develops
3. A **secondary electron bunch** forms and moves toward the plasma surface
4. This bunch emits the secondary attosecond pulse

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.2 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 1990]
  "The formation of a secondary electron bunch which moves towards the plasma surface appears to be primarily driven by the charge imbalance and electrostatic plasma field caused by the penetration of fast electrons"

### Why Similarity Theory Fails for Secondary Pulses

The primary pulse dynamics obey similarity theory (S = N/a₀). But the secondary pulse does **not**:

- The secondary bunch is shielded from the laser field by the primary bunch
- It never reaches relativistic transverse velocities
- Its dynamics are governed by the **non-relativistic plasma frequency** (N only), not S

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.2 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 2000]
  "The dynamics of the second bunch are therefore governed solely by the non-relativistic plasma frequency, i.e. N, rather than by S = N/a₀"

## Deviation from Simple Plasma-Period Prediction

At larger N and a₀, the simple prediction ω_m = √(N/cos³θ_L) begins to diverge. Edwards corrects this by adding a term proportional to a₀/N:

**T/T_L = √(cos³θ_L/N) + 0.05 · a₀/N**

The correction factor 0.05 arises from the displacement δx that the pulse moves in the fraction of the period between primary and secondary pulses.

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.1 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 1974]
  "With T/t_l = p cos³θ/N + 0.05a₀/N, we produce the orange dashed lines"

## Multi-Cycle Pulses

For longer (τ = 20 fs) driving pulses:
- Every secondary pulse appears with the same delay (plasma frequency is relatively constant)
- Same spectral modulation period as single-cycle case
- But not every optical cycle produces a secondary pulse → weaker overall modulations

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.3 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 2004]
  "not every optical cycle produces a secondary pulse, so the overall modulations of the spectra are weaker"

## Implications for Isolated Attosecond Pulses

### Problem
Secondary pulses appear with **sub-femtosecond delay** (~100-500 as). Proposed isolation methods are ineffective for such short separations:
- Single-cycle driving pulses: help but don't eliminate secondary pulses
- Polarization gating: ineffective at sub-fs timescales
- Multi-frequency beams: same issue
- Attosecond lighthouse: spatial separation too small

### Mitigation Strategies
1. **High plasma density at fixed S**: suppresses secondary pulse formation
   - At large N, the secondary pulse delay becomes so small that it overlaps with the primary
2. **Choose a₀/N regime**: a₀/N > 0.12 gives stronger primary than secondary
3. **Spectral identification**: secondary pulses produce characteristic spectral modulations — identifiable in experiment

— Edwards Thesis §4.4.1 [KB: .../thesis.md, line 1959]
  "proposed methods for improving pulse isolation... are not effective for sub-femtosecond pulse separations"

## Spectral Modulation as Diagnostic

The spectral modulation period directly encodes the pulse separation. This can be used as a **diagnostic** in experiments:
- Observe modulation in reflected spectrum
- Extract pulse separation from ω_m
- Infer plasma density from the delay

Li et al. (2024) showed that spectral modulation of high-order harmonics from relativistic plasma mirrors can be attributed to relativistic self-phase modulation, providing a diagnostic of the PM surface dynamics. The harmonic splitting encodes information about the instantaneous PM shape and electron density profile.

— doi: 10.1103/PhysRevE.109.025212
